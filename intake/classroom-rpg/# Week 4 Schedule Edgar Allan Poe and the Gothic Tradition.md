## 4. Key Literary Terms and Vocabulary    
  
### 4.1 Unreliable Narrator    
#### Definition:    
A narrator who cannot be fully trusted to present events truthfully or objectively. This device creates ambiguity, challenging readers to interpret the story independently.    
  
#### Examples:    
- *The Tell-Tale Heart* (Poe) – The narrator’s insistence on his sanity contrasts with his violent actions.    
- *Gone Girl* (Flynn) – Both Amy and Nick mislead the audience through selective narration.    
- *The Great Gatsby* (Fitzgerald) – Nick Carraway’s bias shapes our perception of Gatsby.    
  
#### Discussion Questions    
  
##### Concrete:    
- How does the narrator in *The Tell-Tale Heart* try to convince the reader of his sanity?    
  
##### Exploratory:    
- What literary techniques make him seem unreliable?    
- Do you naturally trust narrators, or do you always question their perspective?    
  
##### Abstract:    
- Why do authors use unreliable narrators?    
  
##### Exploratory:    
- Does this technique make a story more immersive or confusing?    
- Do unreliable narrators create better mysteries or distort meaning?    
  
##### Connecting:    
- Can you think of a time when someone’s story or version of events seemed unreliable?    
  
##### Exploratory:    
- In real life, how do we determine whether someone is telling the truth?    
- How does this influence the way we engage with media and history?    
  
---  
  
### 4.2 Gothic Conventions    
#### Definition:    
Elements such as dark, eerie settings, supernatural elements, psychological tension, and themes of decay and madness, often used to explore human fears and emotions.    
  
#### Examples:    
- *The Cask of Amontillado* (Poe) – The catacombs create an oppressive, claustrophobic atmosphere.    
- *Crimson Peak* (film) – A decaying mansion reflects the secrets of its inhabitants.    
- *Rebecca* (Du Maurier) – Manderley, the grand estate, symbolizes mystery and memory.    
  
#### Discussion Questions    
  
##### Concrete:    
- How does the setting in *The Cask of Amontillado* enhance the story’s mood?    
  
##### Abstract:    
- What psychological impact might dark, confined spaces have on characters?    
  
##### Connecting:    
- Have you ever visited a place that felt eerie or unsettling? What made it feel that way?    
  
---  
  
### 4.3 Irony    
#### Definition:    
A contrast between expectation and reality.    
- **Dramatic Irony:** The audience knows more than the characters.    
- **Situational Irony:** Events turn out differently than expected.    
- **Verbal Irony:** Saying the opposite of what one means.    
  
#### Examples:    
- *The Tell-Tale Heart* – Dramatic irony: The audience knows the narrator is not sane.    
- *The Lottery* (Jackson) – Situational irony: A quaint town tradition turns deadly.    
- *Monty Python and the Holy Grail* – Verbal irony in sarcastic exchanges.    
  
#### Discussion Questions    
  
##### Concrete:    
- Where do we see dramatic irony in Poe’s work?    
  
##### Abstract:    
- How does irony challenge readers to think critically about a story?    
  
##### Connecting:    
- Can you recall a real-life situation where irony played a role?    
  
---  
  
### 4.4 Symbolism    
#### Definition:    
The use of objects, characters, or events to represent deeper meanings.    
  
#### Examples:    
- *The Cask of Amontillado* – The wine symbolizes deception and Montresor’s duplicity.    
- *The Yellow Wallpaper* – The wallpaper symbolizes the protagonist’s confinement.    
- *The Great Gatsby* – The green light represents Gatsby’s unattainable dreams.    
  
#### Discussion Questions    
  
##### Concrete:    
- What does the heart in *The Tell-Tale Heart* symbolize?    
  
##### Abstract:    
- Why do symbols hold such power in literature?    
  
##### Connecting:    
- Have you ever used a symbol or metaphor to express something personal?
